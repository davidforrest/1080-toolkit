Sound in pipes*

(*note: we will discuss wind instruments in class the week of 10/5, including more depth on the theory that is introduced below). Wind instruments are divided into two categories, open ended or close ended. Open ended pipes have both ends open to the air, such as a trumpet, where you blow in one end and the sound emerges from the other end. Close ended pipes have one end closed and the other end open, such as a clarinet, where the end of the pipe is closed off near the mouth piece. Frequencies of sound waves that naturally resonate in pipes are known as harmonics, just as with stringed instruments.

Standing wave patterns are wave patterns produced in a medium when two waves of identical frequencies interfere to produce points along the medium that appear to be standing still. These exist at certain frequencies known as harmonics. There is a clear mathematical relationship between the pipe length and the wavelength of the wave creating the pattern.

Laser cutting

Laser cutting works by focusing a beam of light (laser) to a spot size, power, and wavelength that can cut through materials of interest. The power density (power/spot size) and wavelength are the two most important factors that determines whether a material can be cut or not. For instance, CO2 lasers (10600nm, far IR spectrum) are used to cut most plastics, wood, and other organic materials.  To cut metals, fiber lasers are used and they typically have a wavelength around 1000nm (far IR). Visible lasers can be used also, but more power is required if the wavelength is not in the optimal zone for absorption. If the wavelength is reflected, then no cutting will occur.

To be able to laser cut the pieces for the kit, a 2D CAD file (which is just a vector image) of the outline we wanted to cut was created. The software then interprets this file and generates what is called G-code, a set of instructions that tells the gantry system of the laser cutter how to move. This whole process if fairly simple and user friendly so you can have parts built within minutes (as compared to 3D printing which usually takes hours). For instance, the total amount of laser cutting time for each kit is about 25 minutes.

Machining

Machining is the process of removing material to get the desired shape/form that you want. Machining is one of the older styles of manufacturing processes, however it is constantly being updated and refined. Think of old mill buildings where there were saws powered by a water wheel versus now where equipment can be computer controlled and automated. In both cases the act of removing the material is very similar, but the technology for doing so is much different.

For the PVC flutes segments, I could have drilled each hole one by one on a drill press or with a hand drill, but that would have been 600 holes! Instead, I programmed a milling machine to cut the holes for me and the complete process took under 3 hours.

The procedure:

This week we’ll be making a whistle/flute and experimenting with different “lengths” of pipes. Lengths is in quotations due to the fact that the effective length is determined by whether a hole is covered or not, and note that you have multiple different length pipes. The first step will be to prepare your 3D printed pieces so you get a better sound. Then you’ll experiment with different hole sizes and locations to make a flute that you like. It doesn’t have to have a particular tuning, but you do have to able to show a short demonstration of you playing it.

Prep the 3D printed pieces

    1. Gather all of the pieces you need for the lab: the mouthpiece, the segments, and the bell
    2. If you look at the mouthpiece, you may notice that the area that is supposed to be sharp for the air to pass over has a step. In order to get a better sound out of your flute, you will need to file this to a point.
    3. Using the flat file, gently file back and forth removing a little material at a time. You can test the sound by plugging the PVC flute segment into the mouthpiece and blowing. NOTE: please wash your mouthpiece with soap and water before putting it in your mouth. https://youtu.be/O8Inqk7lJ-o
    4. Next, file or sand the surfaces where the parts plug together for the segments. Don't get rid of too much material otherwise your flute won't stay together. File/sand just enough so the pieces are still snug but you can pull them apart. Each piece has a "seam" where the 3D printing layer started. Remove that first and then see how it fits.
      Video link
