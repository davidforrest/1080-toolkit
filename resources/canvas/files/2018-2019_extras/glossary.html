Glossary

This page will be populated with a glossary that will be constantly updated (stay tuned)

 

Partial: any of the sine waves that make up a complex tone.  Unlike a harmonic, a partial's frequency does not have to be an integer multiple of the base frequency (fundamental) of the tone.

Harmonic: a partial whose frequency is an integer multiple of a tone's fundamental frequency.

Overtone: any partial above the lowest one (the fundamental frequency).

 

Vibration: the physical motion (mechanical, periodic) induced by a force on an object.

Oscillation: a periodically changing quantity that we observe or calculate. For example, we might observe a vibration (say in many directions) and decompose it into oscillations.

Boundary conditions: description of string motion, air pressure, etc. at specific locations on an instrument.

Initial conditions: description of string motion, air pressure, etc. at a specific time, most typically at the onset of a vibration.

Node: point on an instrument or in a medium where there is no motion.

Anti-node: point on an instrument or in a medium where there is maximum motion.

Traveling wave: any vibration pattern that has moving nodes and anti-nodes.

Standing wave: any vibration pattern that produces constant nodes and anti-nodes.

Equilibrium: the state of a system characterized by balance between all forces involved.

Nut/Bridge: on a stringed instrument, the nut is the node furthest from the body and the bridge is the node attached to the body. The nut and bridge form no-displacement boundary conditions.

 

Half-step (aka semitone): distance between two adjacent pitches on the piano.

Cent: 1/100 of a half-step.

Whole-step (aka tone): the distance of two half-steps on the piano (e.g. C to D - there is a black note in between).

 

Interval: relative distance between two pitches (in music). Each integer distance in semitones corresponds has a few interval names. Each interval has a distinctive sound.

      0 half-step : "perfect unison" or "unison"

      1 half-step : "minor second"

      2 half-steps: "major second"

      3 half-steps: "minor third"

      4 half-steps: "major third"

      5 half-steps: "perfect fourth"

      6 half-steps: "augmented fourth" or "diminished fifth"

      7 half-steps: "perfect fifth"

      8 half-steps: "minor sixth"

      9 half-steps: "major sixth"

    10 half-steps: "minor seventh"

    11 half-steps: "major seventh"

    12 half-steps: "perfect octave" or "octave"

Consonant interval: a harmonious sounding interval. Psycho-acoustically, we tend to favor intervals where harmonics of the two pitches involved line up.

Dissonant interval: an inharmonious sounding interval. Harmonics/ overtones of the two pitches tend not to line up, so we hear this as "clashy".

 

Some Synthesis Terms:

MIDI: the standard protocol for communicating basic musical communication in the digital domain: essentially this is a set of rules that got agreed upon in the 80s and has been used in the music technology community ever since! The MIDI protocol is what allows you to plug a musical keyboard into your computer and have software on the computer know which note you want to be played.

Oscillator: Generates the fundamental signal shape fed into the synth  — for example a sine wave, or something more complicated — that is being filtered and later output as sound.

Envelope: Controls the amplitude profile of sounds played by the synth.

Filter: amplifies some frequencies and attenuates others. Recall the low-pass filter, from the first lab assignment, which we used to remove high frequencies from our signal.

VCA: combines and amplifies signals before the sound is finally transduced through speakers.

CV: (in modular synthesis) stands for “control voltage”. Varying signal inputs to one of these inlets can be used to change a parameter of a module dynamically (e.g. the amplitude multiplication factor of a VCA). When an envelope is triggered it generates a continuously varying sequence of control voltages or a control signal.

Sequencer: Automatically generates a repeating (pre-programmed) sequence of notes in time with a pulse (or trigger signal).

Low frequency oscillator (LFO): generates a periodic control voltage, generally at a much lower frequency than the “oscillators” as we have encountered them so far. Recall that a control voltage controls some parameter of another module, e.g. the cutoff of a filter.
