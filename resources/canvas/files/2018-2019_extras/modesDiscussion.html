Of the seven modes we discussed, one is never used, so that leaves six, and two you probably already know: major (Ionian) and natural minor (Aeolian), so that leaves four new ones.  In order, using only the white keys and starting on C:
Ionian (start on C) is simply a major scale.
Dorian (start on D, for Dorian) for many English folk song  (Scarborough Fair).  Also Eleanor Rigby.
Phrygian (start on E) for many Spanish folk songs... 
Lydian (start on F) is a major scale with a sharp four, used in every John Williams film score:
Film Scoring 101 - The Lydian Mode
Mixolydian (start on G) is a major scale with a flat seven.  Think the beginning of "Norwegian Wood" or Blind Melon's "No Rain"
Aeolian (start on A) is just natural minor
Locrian (start on B) is rare enough that you can safely ignore it.

Kate: Two things to consider when deciding on the mode; 

1. What is the key signature (which sharps and flats are used)? 
2. What is the “home” pitch? 
With ABITW, we have no sharps and flats in any of the chords, right? 
BUT the ends of phrases most often return to D and also depart from D. So, starting on D If we build a scale with no sharps and flats, the #6th scale degree implies the Dorian mode.  
When the key signature does not match the “home” pitch (or the tonic), then chances are you are probably working in a mode. If key signature (sharps and flats used) matches the key, then you are in Ionian (major) mode. (This is arguably another side effect of colonialism.... but that’s for a different seminar.)
