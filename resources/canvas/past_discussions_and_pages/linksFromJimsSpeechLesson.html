Links from Jim's speech lesson

A nice piece on vocal tract resonances:

http://clas.mq.edu.au/speech/acoustics/frequency/vocal_tract_resonance.html
Macquarie's site has other speech stuff too:
http://clas.mq.edu.au/speech/acoustics/index.html
That cool set of cross section MRIs of spoken vowels:
http://www.seeingspeech.ac.uk/ipachart/display.php?chart=4&datatype=2&speaker=1
Change "Ultrasound" to "MRI" then click on a vowel.
A map of vowel sounds and the first two formants.  Kind of wishful thinking, but it's a start:
http://www.phy.davidson.edu/fachome/dmb/digitalspeech/formantmap.gif
RTGram, a real-time spectrogram:
http://www.phon.ucl.ac.uk/resource/sfs/rtgram/
Wavesurfer, a simple, but powerful spectrogram and speech visualization tool:
https://sourceforge.net/projects/wavesurfer/
A video showing injection locking of metronomes:
Synchronization of Metronomes
Samples of compressed voice, using Silk:
https://opus-codec.org/examples/
Silk is the voice part of the general-purpose open-source compressor called Opus:
https://opus-codec.org/
If you want to look at the guts of the code to compress/decompress voice and music, Opus is a good place to start.
Rabiner's classic tutorial on Hidden Markov Models:
http://www.cs.ubc.ca/~murphyk/Bayes/rabiner.pdf
This piece explains Hidden Markov and a few other aspects of speech recognition:
https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/speech-getting-computers-understand-overlapping/
A popular open-source speech recognition engine called Sphinx:
http://www.speech.cs.cmu.edu/
An explanation of Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients:
http://www.practicalcryptography.com/miscellaneous/machine-learning/guide-mel-frequency-cepstral-coefficients-mfccs/
